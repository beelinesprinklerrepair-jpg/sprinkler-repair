Why Sprinkler Blowouts Matter in Idaho

When water sits inside an irrigation line during a hard freeze, it expands. PVC pipe, fittings, sprinkler heads, valves and backflow devices are not built to absorb that expansion. The damage usually does not show up until spring, when the system is turned back on and water starts leaking from a cracked fitting, broken head, split valve body or damaged backflow assembly.

A proper sprinkler blowout clears standing water from the system before freezing temperatures hit. That is why blowouts are one of the most important seasonal services for homeowners in the Treasure Valley.

What Beeline Does on Every Sprinkler Blowout

Not all blowouts are done the same way. A good blowout is not just hooking up air and rushing through the zones. The goal is to clear the system without damaging heads, valves, fittings or the backflow device.

Commercial Grade Compressor

We use a commercial grade compressor with the air volume needed to clear residential sprinkler systems properly. Small homeowner compressors usually do not have enough CFM to fully clear long lateral lines.

Every Zone Cleared

Each zone is opened and cleared individually. We run the system zone by zone so water is pushed out through the sprinkler heads instead of left sitting in the lines.

Multiple Passes

Some zones need more than one pass to clear properly. We do not rely on one quick burst of air and leave water sitting 40 or 50 feet out in the yard.

Safe Pressure

The goal is air volume, not reckless pressure. Too much pressure can damage seals, heads and fittings. We use the right approach for the system instead of trying to force it.

Backflow Device Check

The backflow device is one of the most expensive parts to replace if it freezes. We make sure it is drained and left correctly for winter.

Controller Shutdown

Before we leave, we set the controller to off season mode so it does not try to run the system during winter.

The city valve is not the same thing as your private sprinkler shutoff.

Beeline works on the private side of the sprinkler system. We shut down and blow out the homeowner side of the system without relying on city owned valves. If you are not sure where your private shutoff, backflow or blowout connection is located, we can help identify it during the service visit.

Important: Do not turn city irrigation valves yourself. If you are unsure which valve belongs to you, ask before turning anything.

Can I Blow Out My Own Sprinklers?

Some homeowners try to blow out their sprinkler system with a small garage compressor. The problem is that most small compressors can build pressure but do not have enough air volume to keep water moving through long sprinkler lines. That can leave water trapped in low spots, long laterals, manifolds or the backflow device.

The other mistake is using too much pressure. High pressure can damage sprinkler heads, rotor seals, valve parts and fittings. A proper blowout is about controlled air volume, not just blasting the system as hard as possible.

If your system has multiple zones, a backflow device, long runs, slopes, drip zones or an unclear shutoff setup, it is usually better to schedule a professional sprinkler blowout. You can also visit our sprinkler troubleshooting center if you are trying to diagnose issues before or after the season.

What Can Freeze Damage Break?

Skipping a sprinkler blowout can lead to expensive spring repairs. The most common freeze damage we see includes cracked sprinkler heads, split PVC fittings, broken lateral lines, damaged valve diaphragms, cracked backflow devices and leaking manifolds.

Backflow Damage

Backflow assemblies hold water above ground and are especially vulnerable during a hard freeze.

Valve Damage

Water trapped around valves can freeze and damage diaphragms, seals or valve bodies. See: valve box full of water.

Broken Heads

Water left inside sprinkler heads can freeze and crack the body or damage internal parts.

Split Lines and Fittings

PVC pipe and fittings can split when trapped water expands inside the line.

Spring Leaks

A system may look fine all winter but leak when turned back on. Wet spots and low pressure are common symptoms.
