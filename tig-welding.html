Gas tungsten arc welding, explained

The process behind
a clean weld.

TIG welding — gas tungsten arc welding, or GTAW — is the precision end of the trade. A non-consumable tungsten electrode strikes the arc, an inert gas like argon shields the puddle from the air, and filler metal is fed in by hand, one dip at a time. Nothing splatters, nothing crusts over. What you get is a tight, deliberate bead that often needs no cleanup at all.

Because the heat and the filler are controlled separately, a TIG welder can dial in exactly how much energy goes into the metal and exactly how much filler goes down. That control is why TIG is the go-to for stainless, aluminum, thin material, and any weld that's going to be looked at. Idaho Welding Service runs TIG welding services for homeowners, shops, and contractors across the Treasure Valley whenever the job calls for it.

Precision, not speed

TIG trades speed for quality — it's the slow, careful process you choose when the weld has to be right and has to look right. Why TIG is the precision process

One weld puddle, fully under control.

Most welding processes tie the heat and the filler together. TIG splits them apart. The welder holds the arc in one hand and feeds filler rod with the other, often adjusting the amperage in real time with a foot pedal. That independent control over heat and filler is what makes GTAW welding so precise — and why the beads come out clean, even, and nearly spatter-free.

  • Independent control of heat and filler for exact, repeatable beads
  • Clean, low-spatter welds that need little grinding or cleanup
  • Fine heat control that won't warp or burn through thin material
  • AC for aluminum, DC for stainless and steel — settings matched to the metal
  • Cosmetic, visible welds where appearance matters as much as strength

Where TIG earns its keep

What TIG welding is best for.

Stainless, aluminum & thin metal

TIG is built for the metals that give other processes trouble. Aluminum TIG welding runs on AC to break through the oxide layer, and stainless TIG welding in Idaho is exactly the kind of clean, corrosion-safe work the process was made for. The fine heat control lets a TIG welder join thin material without warping or blowing through it, which makes it the right tool for tanks, boxes, food-grade equipment, tubing, and specialty alloy jobs.

Visible & cosmetic welds

Some welds live where nobody looks. Others sit right in plain sight — a railing people run their hand along, a gate at the front of a property, a bracket on something on display. When the weld has to look as good as it holds, TIG is the answer. The smooth, evenly stacked bead reads as finish work, not a repair, so railings and cosmetic pieces come out looking intentional instead of patched.

Straight talk before you book

TIG or MIG? An honest answer.

When MIG is the smarter call

TIG isn't automatically better — it's better for certain jobs. MIG is faster, lays down more metal per minute, and usually costs less, which makes it the sensible choice for a lot of everyday steel repair and heavier fabrication. If a weld is going to be hidden, painted over, or just needs to hold, paying for TIG time is often money that doesn't need to be spent. We'll tell you when MIG or stick will do the job for less.

Mobile TIG, wind & the shop

TIG is worth the extra time and cost when appearance and precision matter — thin metal, stainless, aluminum, and welds people see. One catch on-site: TIG leans on a steady curtain of shielding gas, and even a light breeze can blow that shield away and ruin the weld. So TIG work usually happens back at the shop or somewhere genuinely sheltered on-site. If a piece can't move, we'll look at blocking the wind or switching to a process that holds up outdoors.

Common questions

TIG welding FAQ.

What is TIG welding?

TIG welding — short for gas tungsten arc welding, or GTAW — uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode and a shield of inert gas, usually argon, to lay down a clean, tightly controlled weld. Filler metal is added by hand, separate from the arc, which is what gives TIG its precision and its smooth, low-spatter appearance.

What is TIG welding best for?

TIG shines on stainless steel, aluminum, and thin material, and on any weld that will be seen — railings, food-grade equipment, and cosmetic or specialty work. The fine heat control keeps thin metal from warping or burning through, and the clean beads need little to no cleanup afterward.

TIG or MIG welding — which do I need?

MIG is faster and usually cheaper, and it's the right call for a lot of everyday steel repair. TIG is slower and costs more, but it's worth it when appearance and precision matter — thin metal, stainless, aluminum, and welds people will actually look at. Tell us about the job and we'll tell you honestly which process fits.

Can you TIG weld aluminum and stainless steel?

Yes. Aluminum TIG welding and stainless TIG welding are exactly what the process is built for. TIG runs AC for aluminum to clean the oxide layer and DC for stainless and steel, so the settings get matched to the metal for a strong, clean joint.

Do you offer mobile TIG welding on-site?

Sometimes, with a caveat. TIG depends on a steady shield of gas, and wind blows that shield away, so TIG work usually happens in the shop or somewhere sheltered on-site. If the job has to be welded in place, we'll look at whether the spot can be blocked from the wind or whether MIG or stick is the better on-site choice.
