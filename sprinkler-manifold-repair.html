Sprinkler Manifold Repair Near Me — Treasure Valley

Your sprinkler manifold is where everything comes together — the valves, the PVC fittings, the connections back to the main water supply, and the wiring to the controller. When one part of that assembly fails, you get dead zones, water pooling in the valve box, or zones that won't shut off. It's one of the more common service calls we get across Nampa, Caldwell, and Meridian.

The good news is that most manifold problems don't require replacing the whole assembly. A bad solenoid, a blown diaphragm, or a cracked fitting is usually a straightforward fix. We'll pull the manifold apart, figure out exactly what's failed, and give you a quote on just what needs to be done.

What We Repair

Manifold issues usually fall into a few categories. Solenoid failure is the most common — the electric solenoid on each valve can burn out or get corroded, leaving a zone that won't open or won't close. Diaphragm failure is next — the rubber diaphragm inside each valve wears out over time and causes the zone to either not activate or run continuously. And cracked PVC fittings or risers are common in older systems, especially after a hard freeze.

  • Valve solenoid replacement — zone not responding to controller
  • Diaphragm replacement — zone running constantly or not activating
  • Cracked PVC fitting or riser repair
  • Full manifold rebuild — new valves, PVC, and connections
  • Valve box water leak diagnosis and repair
  • Electrical wire repair and zone tracing

Repair vs. Full Rebuild

In most cases we can repair the specific component that failed — a solenoid here, a diaphragm there — without touching the rest of the manifold. This keeps the cost down and gets the system running same day. If the PVC is cracked in multiple places, the layout is inefficient, or the valves are older than 15 years and starting to fail regularly, a full rebuild starts to make more sense. We'll be straight with you about which option is the right call.

We've done hundreds of manifold repairs across Eagle, Star, Kuna, and Middleton. Whether it's a single failed valve or a manifold that needs to be rebuilt from scratch, we carry the parts and can usually get it done in one visit.

Signs Your Manifold Needs Service

  • Water pooling inside the valve box between watering cycles
  • One or more zones not activating from the controller
  • Zone that won't shut off — runs continuously even after controller is off
  • Clicking or buzzing solenoid with no zone response
  • Visible cracks in PVC pipe or fittings at the manifold
  • Constant low pressure across all zones pointing back to the manifold feed

Common Questions

Sprinkler Manifold Repair FAQ

What is a sprinkler manifold? +
The manifold is the assembly where your irrigation valves are grouped together, usually inside a valve box in the yard. Each valve controls one zone. The manifold connects your main water supply to each zone via PVC fittings and pipes.
How do I know if my manifold needs repair? +
Common signs include water pooling inside the valve box, one or more zones not responding to the controller, cracked or weeping PVC fittings, or a zone that runs continuously. Any of these usually points to a failed valve diaphragm, cracked fitting, or manifold assembly issue.
Can you rebuild a manifold instead of replacing it entirely? +
Yes. In many cases we replace only the failed components — a solenoid, a diaphragm, or a cracked fitting — without touching the rest of the manifold. If the PVC has extensive cracking or the valves are failing regularly, a full rebuild may be the smarter long-term move.
How much does manifold repair cost? +
Valve solenoid or diaphragm replacements typically run $80 to $150 per valve. Full manifold rebuilds with new PVC and valves usually run $300 to $700 depending on the number of zones and parts needed. We give you a quote before starting any work.
Why does my valve box keep filling with water? +
A valve box that keeps filling with water usually has a slow seep from a cracked PVC fitting, a valve that isn't seating properly, or a weeping diaphragm. In some cases groundwater drainage can also be a factor. We'll pull the box apart and find the source.
