SMAW welding, explained

The field-repair
king of welding.

Stick welding is the everyday name for shielded metal arc welding, or SMAW. It works by striking an electric arc between a flux-coated metal rod and the steel being joined. The heat melts both, and as the flux coating burns it releases a shield that keeps the air away from the molten weld, then leaves a layer of slag on top. That slag gets chipped off after — but it's also the reason no separate gas bottle is needed.

That self-shielding design is what makes stick the workhorse of field welding repair. The gear is simple and rugged, the arc holds steady in rough conditions, and it bites through the kind of weathered, painted, or slightly rusty steel you actually find on Idaho farm equipment. For a lot of the repairs Idaho Welding Service handles across the Treasure Valley, it's the first process on the list.

Why stick wins outdoors

Runs anywhere the work is.

MIG and TIG both rely on a curtain of shielding gas, and a light breeze is enough to blow that curtain away and wreck the weld. Stick carries its shielding inside the rod, so wind doesn't beat it. Pair that with engine-driven power on the truck and it becomes the process that goes where the breakdown is — a back pasture, a windy corral, a driveway, a job site with no outlet for miles.

  • Wind-resistant — the flux shields the weld, so a breeze won't ruin it
  • No gas bottle and no wall power needed to lay down a solid bead
  • Bites through rust, mill scale, and paint better than MIG or TIG
  • Handles thick, heavy steel and high-load repairs with deep penetration
  • Simple, rugged equipment that holds up to real field conditions

Outdoor & farm steel

Out in the open, stick is hard to beat. Fence and corral steel, gates, structural repairs, and outdoor brackets get welded right where they stand, wind and all. Because the process doesn't need a gas shield or a controlled shop, we can run it on livestock ground, along a field line, or on a structure already anchored in place — no tearing something down just to move it indoors.

Straight talk on the trade-offs

Where stick fits, and where it doesn't.

The honest limits

No single process wins everywhere, and we'll tell you when stick isn't the right one. Every stick weld leaves slag that has to be chipped and brushed off, so there's more cleanup than a clean MIG bead. It's also not the pick for thin sheet metal, which can burn through, or for cosmetic work where the finished seam needs to look flawless. For those jobs, TIG or MIG is the better tool, and we match the process to the metal rather than forcing one to fit.

Why mobile trucks lean on it

In Idaho weather and farm conditions, most field repairs favor exactly what stick is good at: heavy steel, dirty surfaces, wind, and no shop power. That's why the mobile rig leans on it — it's dependable when the setting is anything but ideal. Common questions

Stick welding FAQ.

What is stick welding?

Stick welding is the everyday name for shielded metal arc welding, or SMAW. An electric arc jumps between a flux-coated metal rod and the steel you're joining, melting both into a strong joint. As the flux burns it shields the weld from the air, so no separate gas bottle is needed. It's the simple, rugged process that built most of the farm and field steel in Idaho.

Why is stick welding good for outdoor and field repairs?

Because the shielding comes from the rod itself, not a gas that a breeze can blow away. MIG and TIG both rely on a gas curtain that wind ruins, which makes them tough to run outside. Stick keeps laying down solid welds in an open field, a windy corral, or a driveway, which is exactly why mobile rigs lean on it across the Treasure Valley.

What is stick welding best used for?

Heavy, dirty, real-world steel. Farm and ranch equipment, cracked buckets and attachment mounts, trailer frames, fence and corral steel, gates, and structural repairs are all right in its wheelhouse. Stick handles thick material and less-than-perfect surfaces better than most processes, so it's the first pick when a repair has to hold up under load and weather.

Can you stick weld rusty or dirty steel?

Better than the alternatives, yes. We still knock off heavy scale, rust, and grease so the weld bonds right, but stick is forgiving on the kind of weathered, painted, or slightly dirty steel you find on farm equipment and old repairs. That tolerance is a big part of why it's the go-to for field work where a spotless surface isn't realistic.

What are the downsides of stick welding?

Every stick weld leaves a layer of slag that has to be chipped and brushed off, so there's more cleanup than a clean MIG or TIG bead. It's also not the right call for thin sheet metal, which can burn through, or for cosmetic work where the finish has to look seamless. For those jobs we reach for MIG or TIG instead and match the process to the metal.

Do you offer mobile stick welding across the Treasure Valley?

Yes. Stick is the backbone of what the truck does. Because the rig carries its own engine-driven welding power and the process shrugs off wind and weather, we can run stick repairs on farms, job sites, and properties from Boise to Caldwell with no outlet in sight.
