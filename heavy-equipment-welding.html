Common heavy equipment repairs

The breaks we fix over and over.

Working equipment fails in predictable places — the high-stress corners, the wear edges, and the pins and mounts that take the load every cycle. Most heavy equipment welding calls fall into a short list, and none of them need a trip to a shop to diagnose.

  • Excavator and loader bucket repair — cracked shells, worn cutting edges, and loose teeth adapters
  • Cracked attachment mounts, quick-attach plates, and bracket ears
  • Thumb and blade repair, plus pin and bushing bore build-up
  • Boom and stick cracks welded out and reinforced with gussets
  • Skid steer attachment repair — buckets, forks, grapples, and plates
  • Reinforcement and gusseting on anything that keeps cracking in the same spot

Two worlds, same welder

Construction iron and farm steel.

Construction & excavation equipment

On a job site, buckets take the worst of it — cracked shells, worn cutting edges, and teeth adapters that shear off in rocky Idaho ground. Beyond buckets, it is cracked quick-attach plates, broken attachment mounts, thumb and blade repairs, and cracks that open up in booms and sticks where the steel flexes every cycle. We weld the crack out, then gusset or plate the high-stress areas so the repair outlasts the last one. Ask about wear plate and hardfacing for edges that grind down fast.

Farm & ag equipment

Farm equipment welding repair is its own animal — loader arms and bucket edges worn thin, hay equipment that flexes and cracks, implements and cultivators that hit rocks, and three-point hitches and drawbars that take a beating. When a machine goes down in the middle of a season, hauling it in is not an option. We come to the farm, weld it in the field, and reinforce the parts that keep failing so you can get back to work before the weather turns.

Why heavy repairs are different

Thick steel, hard use, no second chances.

The right process for the conditions

Heavy equipment welding rarely happens in a clean shop. Wind, thick plate, and awkward positions are the normal working conditions — so process choice matters. Stick and flux-core welding hold up outdoors where shielding gas would blow away, MIG earns its keep on cleaner shop repairs, and the machine on the truck runs all of them, so the job gets the process that fits the conditions instead of the one that's convenient. Welding power rides along too — a machine dead in the middle of a field is exactly the kind of call this service exists for.

Fix the crack, not the symptom

A crack in a bucket or attachment mount didn't start where you can see it. A repair that lasts starts with grinding or gouging the crack out completely and keeping it from running further — then welding with filler metal matched to the steel and the loads the part actually carries, for strength and impact toughness, not just looks. Where wear is the real problem, ask about wear plate and reinforcement so the same failure doesn't come back next season.

Common questions

Heavy equipment welding FAQ.

What kinds of heavy equipment do you weld?

Excavators, wheel loaders, backhoes, skid steers, dozers, and their attachments, plus farm equipment like tractor loader arms, hay equipment, implements, and three-point hitches. If it is built out of steel and it is broken, cracked, or worn, it is worth a photo and a phone call.

Can you fix my machine in the field if it cannot move?

Yes. Field welding repair is a big part of this work. When a machine is down in a pit, a field, or the middle of a job site and can not be driven or loaded, we bring the welder and the steel to the machine and fix it where it sits so you are not paying to drag a dead machine across the valley.

What are the most common heavy equipment repairs you do?

Bucket repair leads the list — cracked shells, worn cutting edges, and loose or broken teeth adapters. After that it is cracked attachment mounts and quick-attach plates, thumb and blade repairs, cracks in booms and sticks, and reinforcing high-stress areas with gussets before they tear again.

How much does heavy equipment welding cost?

It depends on the machine, the metal, the access, and how much weld time the repair takes. The fastest way to get a real number is to text photos of the damage and your location to 208-409-0756. You get a straight answer on the likely fix before any work starts.

Do you repair farm equipment too?

Yes. Loader arms, bucket edges, hay equipment, implements, three-point hitches, and worn or cracked frames are common farm equipment welding repair calls. When a machine is down in the middle of a season, we come to the farm and get it back to work.

Can you reinforce a bucket or mount so it holds up better?

Often, yes. If a bucket, mount, or bracket keeps cracking in the same place, the fix is more than a bead — it is welding the crack out and adding gussets or plate to spread the load. Ask about wear plate and hardfacing options for edges and high-wear areas so the repair lasts longer than the last one.
